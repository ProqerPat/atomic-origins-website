The Day the Sun Rose Twice

The Trinity Test was the first detonation of a nuclear device, carried out on July 16, 1945, in the New Mexico desert. The test was designed to determine whether the research of the Manhattan Project could actually be used in a functional weapon, in order to change the tide of WWII. The explosion rose into a fireball and towering mushroom cloud that made the power of nuclear energy one of a kind.

  1. 5:29 a.m. - The 6.2 kg plutonium core initiated its fission reaction, releasing around 20 kilotons of TNT equivalent energy.
  2. 0.10 ms - A blinding fireball formed, hitting temperatures of over 11 million degrees celcius in it's core.
  3. 0.66 ms - The shockwave expanded outward at supersonic speeds, generating extreme pressure and heat, digging a 335 m wide crater.
  4. 1.20 ms - The fireball touched the desert floor, vaporizing sand into radioactive glass called trinitite.
  5. 2.00 s - The mushroom cloud continues to rise rapidly and reached a staggering diameter of 200 m.
  6. 40 s - The shock wave reached observers, felt over 160 km away, while the mushroom cloud climbed to a total of 12.1 km in height.
Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds.

- J. Robert Oppenheimer

The impact of the Trinity Test goes far beyond the New Mexico desert, uniting the scientific community around a shared breakthrough. Its consequences still guide international policy and future technological development.
